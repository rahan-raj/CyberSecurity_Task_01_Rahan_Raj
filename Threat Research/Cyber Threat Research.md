##  Cyber Threat Research

### 1.  Phishing

**Definition:**
Phishing is a social engineering attack where cybercriminals impersonate trusted entities (banks, tech companies, colleagues) through fake emails, messages, or websites to trick users into revealing sensitive information such as passwords or credit card numbers.

**Real-World Example:**
In 2020, Twitter suffered a major breach when attackers used spear-phishing calls to trick Twitter employees into handing over their credentials, leading to the takeover of high-profile accounts including Barack Obama and Elon Musk, which were then used to run a Bitcoin scam.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Always verify the sender's email address carefully before clicking any links.
- Never enter credentials on a site you reached through an email link — go directly to the website.
- Enable MFA so that stolen passwords alone are not enough to access your account.
- Use an email provider with built-in spam and phishing filters.

---

### 2.  Malware

**Definition:**
Malware (malicious software) is any program intentionally designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to a computer system. It includes viruses, trojans, spyware, adware, and worms.

**Real-World Example:**
The **Emotet** malware, first discovered in 2014, started as a banking trojan and evolved into one of the most dangerous malware delivery platforms in the world. It spread through phishing emails and infected millions of systems globally before being taken down by law enforcement in 2021.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Keep your operating system and all software updated to patch known vulnerabilities.
- Install reputable antivirus software and keep its definitions updated.
- Do not download attachments or software from untrusted sources.
- Avoid clicking suspicious links, even from known contacts.

---

### 3.  Ransomware

**Definition:**
Ransomware is a type of malware that encrypts a victim's files or entire system, making them inaccessible. The attacker then demands a ransom payment (usually in cryptocurrency) in exchange for the decryption key.

**Real-World Example:**
In May 2021, the **Colonial Pipeline** attack in the USA saw hackers deploy ransomware that forced the company to shut down a major fuel pipeline supplying the US East Coast. The company paid approximately $4.4 million in ransom to restore operations.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Regularly back up important data to an offline or cloud location not connected to your main system.
- Never open unexpected email attachments or click suspicious links.
- Keep all software patched and up to date.
- Use endpoint protection tools that can detect ransomware behavior before it executes.

---

### 4.  Social Engineering

**Definition:**
Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or revealing confidential information. Instead of hacking systems directly, attackers exploit human trust, fear, urgency, or authority.

**Real-World Example:**
In 2019, a hacker tricked a GoDaddy employee using social engineering techniques to transfer control of customer cryptocurrency domains. The attacker simply called support, impersonated the account owner, and convinced the staff member to make the changes.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Always verify the identity of anyone requesting sensitive information, even if they claim authority.
- Be skeptical of urgent requests — attackers deliberately create time pressure to prevent careful thinking.
- Organizations should train employees regularly on recognizing social engineering tactics.
- Implement a verification protocol before acting on sensitive requests (e.g., call back on a known number).

---

### 5.  Data Breach

**Definition:**
A data breach is an incident where unauthorized individuals gain access to confidential, protected, or sensitive data. This can include personal information, financial records, passwords, or intellectual property.

**Real-World Example:**
In 2021, the **Facebook data breach** exposed the personal data of over 533 million users across 106 countries, including phone numbers, full names, locations, and email addresses. The data was made available for free on hacking forums.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Use unique, strong passwords for every account so one breach does not compromise others.
- Enable MFA on all accounts to add a second layer of protection.
- Regularly check services like [HaveIBeenPwned](https://haveibeenpwned.com) to see if your data has been exposed.
- Organizations must follow data minimization principles and encrypt stored user data.
